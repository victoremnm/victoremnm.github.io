> **I've realized that I’ve been hiding behind my usefulness.**

On paper, I look like a responsible, thoughtful leader: I manage up, manage down, think about emotional dynamics, and keep things from blowing up. In conversations, I’m the one who listens, asks good questions, helps people think through their problems, and tries to make them feel better. People experience me as helpful, curious, and steady.

But underneath that, there’s a pattern I’m only now starting to see clearly: I rarely let people actually know me.

I don’t share much about my own anxiety, exhaustion, or confusion. I don’t talk in detail about how I got from where I was to where I am now, what it cost me, or what I’m afraid of. I focus almost exclusively on other people’s stories and other people’s needs. That has become my identity: the one who helps, the one who holds, the one who listens.

This didn’t come out of nowhere. For a long time, I haven’t really felt deeply believed in or fully seen. There weren’t many examples in my life where someone consistently showed up and said,
"I want to understand \*you\*—your journey, your doubts, your decisions—because you matter to me."

So I adapted. I learned that it was safer to pull those parts of myself back and lead with what I could do for others instead.

A former colleague said something that landed as minimizing—like they only knew an outdated, limited version of me. I gave the bare minimum answer about what I do now and then carried the anger and disappointment privately. I didn’t invite them into my story. I didn’t say, “That actually stings” or "There’s more to this journey, want to hear it?"

I stayed protected and closed, and then I blamed them (internally) for not understanding.

The hard truth is: I often feel misunderstood while also actively preventing people from having the information and emotional access they’d need to understand me.

I’ve also been proud of not needing much from people—of being the one who supports instead of the one who needs support. But that pride is double‑edged. It keeps me safe, and it also keeps me lonely.

My major learning from this conversation is that this hiding—staying in helper mode, refusing to reveal much about who I am and what I’m going through—is no longer serving me. It protects me from some pain, but it also blocks the kind of connection and belief I say I want.

I’m not suddenly going to spill my guts to everyone. That would feel fake and unsafe. But I can start making deliberate, small choices in specific relationships:

-   Answer one question from my family or a close friend more honestly than I usually would.
-   When someone says something that hurts, name it gently instead of swallowing it.
-   When there’s an opening to tell my story, take up a bit more space instead of defaulting to “I’m fine” or changing the subject.

The shift I’m aiming for is subtle but significant: from being only the competent, helpful listener to also allowing myself to be a visible, vulnerable person. That means accepting the risk that some people won’t handle it well—but also opening the door for some people to actually see me, believe in me, and meet me where I really am.
