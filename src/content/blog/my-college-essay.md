With knees weak, arms heavy, palms sweaty, I am calm and ready. As I walk toward the wrestling mat, I am given six minutes to battle against an opponent who has trained for months to defeat me.

The first period is won by whoever starts the match with the best technical preparation. The whistle blows and I'm given two minutes to read my opponent's skill. As I lunge toward a miscalculated takedown, my opponent sprawls and neutrality is instantly compromised, smashing my head to the ground. I find myself struggling to balance the scales back in my favor.

Some competitors begin with advantages while others start from an unfavorable position. I grew up with the latter circumstances. Since kindergarten, I'd rise earlier than the sun to take the bus to a school that was thirty miles away from my impoverished neighborhood. Looking out the tinted window of my seat, I spent many moments reflecting upon my background. I knew that with my thrift-store clothes and dilapidated lunchbox, not only was I different but also I was underprivileged. I am not rich. I don't have well-educated parents. Through school, I seek to overcome my disadvantaged situation.

The second period is won by the better conditioned wrestler. My endurance gained from those practices comes to the fore in this part of the match. Once the whistle blows, I strike back from the bottom, surmounting my formidable opponent, returning to neutral and continuing the fight on an even footing.

Conditioning has also shaped my approach to life outside of wrestling. In fourth grade, I attended summer school due to bad grades for four consecutive years because of my laziness. Then, I entered Tae Kwon Do. Through endless drills and sparring, my teacher, who was also a former marine drill instructor, trained me according to the values of discipline. Though I suffered from the pain, I never gave up. Since then, I never attended summer school and never accepted mediocrity.

The third period, the final period, is won by the wrestler who has the stronger motivation because he is fighting on behalf of something greater than himself. This last period is lonely. My body has abandoned me. My heart has stopped beating. However, my team is still here. Though wrestling is an individual sport, with no assists and no substitutions, I fight for them.

Until I entered wrestling, I never worked to benefit anyone but myself. My team includes my family, friends, mentors, and community. Through their nurturing, I gain the strength to surmount life's obstacles. Because of their efforts, I knew I had to contribute back. I began giving to the community by tutoring students after school and instructing children as a teacher assistant. While I teach them, they inspire me to do better. Deep within themselves, they wrestle right along side me, feeling the elation of victory as if it were their own. The whistle blows and I struggle for the final escape, the final takedown, and the final pin. We are all teammates. We are all wrestlers.

Life is a constant stream of matches between me and my obstacles. Until my final contest, I will relentlessly train and fight these battles, striving to be better than I was the day before.
