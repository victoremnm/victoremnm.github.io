# Personal Charter, 2017-2025

I have been collecting notes for years. Not to romanticize the past, but to understand my own reason for living. This is a short professional update of how my personal charter seems to have evolved from 2017 through 2025, and where I still see blind spots. It is written from memory and notes, so it is incomplete, but the patterns feel consistent enough to be useful.

## The arc in brief

If I compress the timeline, it reads like this: early questioning and exploration, a high‑intensity pivot year, a pandemic‑era reckoning, then a stretch of stabilization followed by a quieter, more integrated phase. I moved cities, changed roles, and learned what happens when intensity fades and routine takes over. The pattern matters more than the events. Early years were marked by uncertainty and a desire for external validation. Later years leaned toward clearer standards and a preference for depth over breadth.

A few concrete markers help anchor that arc. In 2017 and 2018, I was still trying to find acceptance at work and was more reactive about what came next. In 2019, I moved and joined a new team that demanded a different level of ownership, which pulled a lot of reflection to the surface. In 2020, the pandemic forced a more direct look at stress and mental health. I was coping with the ennui of my own loneliness and pointlessness of my time at my role, and funneled it unhealthily to extracurriculars to fill that void. By 2022, I had landed in a role that finally felt stable (or at least I thought), and the need to process everything externally dropped. I actually worked harder than I should have, and lost sleep over things that didn't really matter if I didn't let my achieving self take over my own self-care. The later years felt quieter, but not empty - more integrated, more intentional, and less performative. I stepped into a role that allowed me to pivot and go deeper in a domain. However, when I stopped learning, I knew it was time to go. And here I am today in 2026 where I was thrust into management and had to rely on my soft skills as a core skill versus a nice-to-have as an individual contributor.

## Here's what stayed consistent

A few principles show up again and again in the notes, even as the details change.

- **Identity feels constructed and not found** I tend to think people build themselves through deliberate iteration. That framing has helped me take ownership in work and in life, especially during pivots. In practice, it meant treating each move - new city, new team, new responsibilities - as input to refine how I work and what I care about.

- **Self‑worth seems foundational.** When it was unstable, other parts of life felt unstable too. As it improved, standards and clarity seemed to follow. I can see this most clearly in how I handled rejection and uncertainty: earlier years were full of second‑guessing, later years were more selective and less reactive.

- **Selectivity matters.** Saying yes to fewer projects, communities, and commitments made the right choices clearer. The goal was not excellence, but rather, congruence. Over time, I started choosing roles where I could build systems, not just deliver outputs, which reduced the mismatch between my strengths and my day‑to‑day work.

- **Solitude helps.** Time alone often clarified signal from noise, especially when everything else felt loud. There were stretches where the best thinking happened off the calendar and away from the group chat. That habit became more important as responsibilities increased.

- **Agency is a bias.** I default to inventorying what I can influence. It is a bias toward action and accountability that has helped in leadership moments. When things went sideways, I tended to focus on what I could change in the process rather than what should have gone differently in the environment.

- **Clarity before commitment, when possible.** I try not to force big decisions in a fog. When clarity shows up, commitment tends to follow with less friction. This has not always been possible, but it has been a useful north star for career and relationship decisions.

## Here's what changed over time

The principles above were steady, but how they showed up shifted. Early on, I was more reactive about work and more willing to tolerate misalignment. Around a major pivot, I started to prioritize roles and teams that felt sustainable, not just impressive. The pandemic forced a more direct look at stress and mental health; what used to be implicit became explicit. Relationships followed a similar arc. Early years were background noise, later years were more intentional, and the standards became clearer.

One signal I trust is how often I felt the need to process things externally. During high‑intensity years, I wrote more and sought more feedback. As stability increased, the volume dropped. That is not proof of improvement, but it does suggest that clarity reduced noise. Another signal was decision speed. Early on, I would over‑analyze; later, I could decide faster because the criteria were clearer.

I was desperately concerned of self-preservation, and being good enough. I was also fighting some internal feelings of inadequacies, constantly bringing up my own projections that I'm not worthy (technical) enough. It was never enough, and I realized with my role, I'm going to be forced to continue learning. As I was able to take a look at my rainy day fund, I felt like I bought myself freedom if I need to break the glass. I can finally choose my own destiny instead of being beholden and blinded by total compensation or prestige to match my own perception from peers. 

## Where the gaps still are

A draft is only useful if it acknowledges its blind spots. Reviewing the notes surfaces a few tensions that still deserve attention.

- **Construction can drift into optimization.** If identity is always under construction, when do you rest? There is a risk of treating life like a perpetual build pipeline. I want to preserve agency without making self‑improvement the only lens. I still catch myself evaluating whether I am doing enough instead of asking whether I am doing what matters.

- **Self‑worth needs an anchor.** Self‑worth built on achievement and external validation can be fragile. The open question is what holds when achievement is absent. This shows up when external signals are noisy or when the next milestone is unclear.

- **Solitude can become armor.** It is clarifying, but it can also become a way to avoid vulnerability. The distinction is subtle. I have to notice when I am choosing solitude to think and rest versus choosing it to disappear and hide.

- **Selectivity can become rigidity.** Standards help, but they can harden into avoidance. The work is keeping standards while staying open to growth through discomfort. I want to keep discernment without turning it into a veto.

- **Responsibility has limits.** Agency is real, but not unlimited. The world includes constraints and randomness. I need to hold responsibility and humility at the same time. Over‑indexing on agency can turn into blame when the real issue is structural or external.

- **Clarity is not always a prerequisite.** Some decisions only become clear after you make them. Over‑indexing on clarity can become a form of delay. I am still learning when to wait for the fog to lift and when to move anyway.

## What I carry forward

The most useful version of this charter is not a rigid doctrine. It is a set of principles I test against reality and revise when necessary. If I had to summarize the version I want to carry into the next phase, it would be this: construct without over‑optimizing, choose standards without dimming my own acceptance for randomness and serendipity, own agency while accepting its limits to what I can't control, and build a life that still belongs to something beyond me.

This is a professional update of my own constitution or strategic plan. The point is not to overshare, but to name the evolution of how I make decisions. The last eight years were mostly about moving from reaction to intention. The next phase is about keeping that intention without turning it into a cage.
