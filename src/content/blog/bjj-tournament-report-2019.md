So people were asking how I did during my tournament at Brazilian Jiu Jitsu. Keep in mind this was just a local tournament. I was guaranteed four rounds of round robin matches against opponents. Nothing too special. Just an opportunity for me to get my feet wet.

For that reason, I tried to not take it lightly. But for me, there was a ton of unknowns and questions I wanted to answer. After around ten months of practice at the toughest gyms in the world, how do I square up? As an ex-wrestler who is familiar with the struggle and the fight, how do I match up? Was it worth all of this time? This tournament was my first quiz.

Alright, enough of the tension. Here is the skinny that I give most people. I lost all of my matches in that tournament. That's what I usually tell people if I didn't care about their opinion as they wouldn't understand.

## The Lead-Up

My long answer is a bit more nuanced. I had a pretty tough time preparing for this tournament. I wanted to have a good week where I was consistently working in practice. My bracket was lightweight, which was 155 pounds. No biggie, but the issue was that I was weighing on a scale that was two pounds off. Therefore, I had to really change my methodology to make weight, and opted to experiment with a water retention cut.

The water retention cut involved me carbo loading, water loading, and sodium loading on the first three days of the week to then cutting water and going keto completely for the last two days. In case that failed, I could have committed to an Epsom salt bath and laxatives, but those were options I didn't want to entertain.

To make matters worse, I had many major complications. That week was the week of the Disney+ launch, a very momentous occasion for all teams working on it. It was also momentous because it was a shitshow. Multiple teams had to commit to an on-call schedule. Following the Tuesday that I was on call, I went to practice and got injured with what was later known to be a trapezius muscle strain. I also cut down my calories, so it made living so much worse.

## The Matches

The water cut was successful into getting me into the desired weight class. I weighed in at 154.6, so that was good news. I then proceeded to carbo load and drank some pedialyte. The water cut recovery plan was largely unsuccessful.

### Match 1: Clockwork BJJ

This was an interesting match because I can tell during the moment we grip fought, he was familiar with the grips. He attempted a couple shots and was aggressive, and surprised me with a high single. I got taken down, but I immediately braced for it and got into guard.

Overall, the majority of the match was me fighting at a deficit of two points. I was clinging on to dear life with a cross collar grip as he was fighting to base back up and break my guard. I wanted to consider other options like De La Riva and Spider Guard, but was far too concerned to risk it. There were moments that he got me in half guard and was able to break some of it, but he never passed.

Eventually, after various attempts of closed guard breaks, he was able to get back up and we fought in neutral position. I was ready for his shots, so I was able to sprawl against them. At the final moments, I was able to muster energy, I pulled guard and attempted to go for a scissor sweep against my opponent. Time was called, and I lost 0-2.

**Post Match 1:** My mouth was a desert. I went to my pedialyte bottle right after the match attempting to rehydrate myself. We were reconsidering my game. My top game wasn't great.

### Match 2

This guy was a bit weaker, and certainly didn't wrestle like the other guy, so I thought it was a safe idea to tie up. We did, and he was attempting the typical forward judo trip. I was able to inch back and not get taken down.

However, my energy was shot. My grips were failing. I kept on going for a same side or cross collar with sometimes fishhook grips, but this time, unlike my last match where I didn't feel confident in my shots, I didn't feel like I have the energy to take my shots.

Eventually, after around two minutes of standstill grip fighting, he did a double pull, yanking and hyper extending my elbow. I yelped back in a bit of pain, but kept trudging forward. He attempted another forward trip, and this time, he was successful. Where was my energy? He was able to get to mount and was able to get an arm triangle, but thankfully, it was near the edge of the stage, so I inched backward to go out of bounds. I forgot the moment, but I was able to get a sweep on him. Either way, I lost 2-4.

**Post Match 2:** Grip fighting is not working. I was too exhausted to take shots. I had to rethink things. Not only that, but my left elbow got hyperextended twice, so it just made matters worse.

### Match 3

I came in with a different strategy. I was planning on pulling guard, and so I did. However, instead of placing my foot on the opposite end of my hand grip, I did the opposite and I got passed. That was perfect. Three points lost in the first thirty seconds of the match.

I was able to reestablish myself as a reversal in his closed guard. He got a weak triangle attempt on me that I was able to break, stack him, and went for a pass. There was another time where we got in the same position, and I worked to stack him as well, but didn't control his hips. I was drained.

As a result, we ended the match 3-3. I tied my belt, stood proudly, saw the scorer cursor hover over my name, and felt the ref's hand raise for my opponent. I lost.

**Post Match 3:** The next match would have been against a teammate. My left elbow was beat, and I was parched. I decided to throw the towel and forfeit with the hope that my teammate would win.

## What I Learned

So there you had it. I lost all of my matches. Excuses and extenuating circumstances aside, here is what I learned:

- **I need to improve my neutral and early game.** I know very little about how to grip fight strategically and set up shots. While I was a wrestler and could default to instinct, it's not enough to go in cold.
- **I played far too often not to lose, even when losing.** I was unwilling to take risks and thus defaulted to guards that were substandard but low risk, specifically closed guard and half guard. I applied none of the things I learned in class.
- **I needed to act more and not burn time.** As a teammate told me what our professor told us, it's not a chess match, it's a race for points.

Moving forward, I think I won't do a water cut. I'll roll up. I'll have more energy. I'll take more risks. I'll not waste time. I'll attack first.
