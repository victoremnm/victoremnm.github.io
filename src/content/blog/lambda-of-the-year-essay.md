Just yesterday before I wrote this essay, I visited the gravestone of my grandfather in San Diego. During that moment where I laid flowers on his grave, I reflected on how four years ago, I was clothed orange, hands clasped, brow furrowed and head shaved as I watched the casket of my grandfather being lowered next to my great grandmother who passed away when I was four.

Being a monk was a rite of passage for a Khmer male, a tradition that stayed with my family even after the extirpation of culture during the rise of the Khmer Rouge regime in the 1970s. Death was a concept that I learned extremely early in my childhood, especially because my family frequently shared their stories of the conditions from the concentration camps over the hushed conversations with my relatives.

Growing up in a predominantly black and Hispanic low-income neighborhood, it was difficult for me to relate or share my Khmer culture. When I entered class as a senior with my shaved head and eyebrows, I was greeted with insensitive remarks from classmates that I looked like a skinhead. I failed to disclose to my peers that I became a monk for my grandfather who passed away because I was ashamed of being a Khmer American. I was full of self-hatred for my own Asian culture.

As I burned incense and prayed, I uttered under my breath the scriptures from the dead language that I never knew, attempting to seek meaning from it when it brought me emptiness during the funeral procession four years ago.

---

I cannot say that I have found meaning in my monkhood, but I can say that I have been more open to understanding my culture and identity as a college student. I am grateful that Lambda Phi Epsilon has brought me the opportunity to challenge my Asian American male identity. Because I had brothers who either joined me in this effort or have supported me in inquiring more about my identity, I was encouraged to join the Khmer Student Association, Asian American Wellness Program, and classes in Asian American studies. I continued these efforts as a brother of Lambda Phi Epsilon by assisting in the Listen to the Silence Conference on American issues and co-leading Identity Xpression with my chapter.

Being a part of Lambda Phi Epsilon has brought forward a new vision for me: "to be leaders among men." I learned through the events I organized as a brother and group projects as a student of Management Science & Engineering, I must know how to define good and replicable practices, how to structure organizations, and how to influence and mobilize people. As a brother, I saw how important it was to recognize and differentiate trends in cultures to improve decision making. I saw how subtle and how intricate it was to structure incentives and organizational environments properly, so that teams are equipped to maximize their productivity. I saw how coordinating teams also meant supporting the various interests across individuals and organizations.

I wanted to be that leader among men because I didn't have that role model growing up. I saw that there was opportunity in myself and my brothers to be those heroes. I hope to continue understanding my ability to operate organizations as outgoing president of Lambda Phi Epsilon and incoming Master's student in Management Science & Engineering. I hope to then be able to contribute back to the fraternity by providing the structure for the upcoming years.

---

Leaving the gravesite, I remembered the moments driving 8 hours straight with my father to Stanford University for the first time. My father told me about how proud he was of me. He told me that while I am able to do whatever I want, he wanted me to write his story and the events that transpired during the Khmer Rouge regime. Before he passes away, I promise to record my family's history and understand my identity as a Khmer American. I then hope to write the history of Lambda Phi Epsilon and continue shaping it as a premiere organization that has transformed me to the person I am today.

In sum, the fraternity has allowed me to be more honest with my identity and aspirations. Being able to have brothers who confront challenges by my side as well as brothers who support my efforts in philanthropy has been immeasurably invaluable to my development as a brother, student, and son. I hope to continue giving back to the fraternity as an alumnus of Stanford Lambda Phi Epsilon. In all of these efforts, I hope to be that leader among men for my brothers and my family.
